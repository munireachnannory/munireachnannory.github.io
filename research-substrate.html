Substrate Effect & Thin-Film Correction

When an indenter penetrates a thin film, the underlying substrate distorts the measured hardness and modulus. This work builds a systematic FEM-based framework to quantify, map, and correct for this error across a wide range of film–substrate combinations.

Motivation

Thin-film nanoindentation is used to measure mechanical properties — hardness (H) and elastic modulus (E) — of coatings and surface layers as thin as tens of nanometers. However, when the indentation depth exceeds roughly 10% of the film thickness (the so-called "10% rule"), the measurement is no longer purely representative of the film: the substrate underneath begins to influence the load–displacement response.

This substrate effect leads to systematic over- or under-estimation of material properties depending on whether the substrate is harder or softer than the film. Existing analytical correction models (e.g., Doerner-Nix, Gao, Bec) rely on simplifying assumptions that break down at large indentation depths or for highly mismatched film–substrate systems.

Core problem

The 10% rule is not universally reliable. For very thin films (<200 nm) or extreme property mismatch, significant substrate influence appears well before 10% depth — yet existing correction models fail to capture this. A data-driven correction framework is needed.

Approach

01

Parametric FEM study

Build 2D axisymmetric Abaqus/CAE models covering a broad parameter space: film thickness (50–500 nm), E and H ratios (soft-on-hard and hard-on-soft), indentation depth ratios (h/t = 0.05–0.50), and indenter geometry (spherical and Berkovich-equivalent conical).

02

Sensitivity mapping

For each parameter combination, extract the measured H and E values and compute the percent error relative to the "true" film-only properties. Map these errors as a function of normalized depth h/t across both regimes.

03

ML correction model

Train a neural network surrogate on the FEM dataset to predict the substrate-induced error given observable inputs (indentation depth, load–displacement curve features, estimated modulus mismatch). The model enables real-time correction of experimental measurements.

04

Experimental validation

Validate the correction framework against Cu, Mo, and W thin-film nanoindentation experiments. Compare corrected predictions against reference bulk values to quantify improvement over existing analytical models.
